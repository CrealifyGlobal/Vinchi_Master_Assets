[[Class_Gains]]

Class Name:
Gains

Behavioral Purpose:
The **Gains** class captures the **positive outcomes, improvements, or benefits** customers expect, desire, or experience when interacting with a product, service, or solution. It helps systems identify **value creation opportunities**, optimize **customer satisfaction strategies**, and differentiate **competitive positioning**.

Core Behaviors:
- **Benefit Identification:** Encodes the emotional, functional, social, and financial outcomes that resonate with customer goals.
- **Outcome Mapping:** Links gains to the Customer Jobs they satisfy and the Offerings or Value Propositions that enable them.
- **Impact Evaluation:** Assesses the significance and frequency of gains in driving satisfaction, loyalty, and retention.
- **Experience Tracing:** Measures how gains manifest throughout the customer journey and contribute to holistic satisfaction.
- **Strategy Support:** Provides feedback loops for product, marketing, and experience design based on realized vs. promised gains.

Interaction Targets:
- **Customer Jobs** – Gains emerge as results of successfully completed jobs.
- **Value Proposition** – Defines the intended benefit delivery logic for each gain.
- **Offering** – Shows which product or service materially delivers the gain.
- **Customer Relationships** – Highlights the relational context through which gains are reinforced (e.g., ongoing support, subscription engagement).
- **Customer Satisfaction** – Receives input from realized gains to calculate emotional and experiential performance.

Sensing Triggers:
- Positive feedback (e.g., customer reviews, NPS comments).
- Feature adoption or usage spikes.
- Renewal behavior in subscription models.
- Brand advocacy or organic referral activity.
- Survey responses reflecting outcome achievement.

Decision Rules:
- A Gain must be **linked to at least one Customer Job** and **enabled by at least one Value Proposition**.
- Gains with **high impact scores** should influence prioritization in product and service development.
- Gains may be **multi-dimensional**, spanning more than one gain type (e.g., functional + emotional).
- Gains must be **reflected in offering positioning, messaging, and UX touchpoints**.
- Gains can be used to **triage underperforming offerings** based on satisfaction deltas.

Adaptation Behavior:
- **Emergent Amplification:** Frequently realized and highly rated gains are candidates for marketing emphasis or new value propositions.
- **Gap Analysis:** Absence of expected gains in delivered outcomes signals experience misalignment.
- **Segment-Specific Tuning:** Gains vary in importance across customer segments and can be weighted accordingly.
- **Life-Cycle Sensitivity:** Some gains grow in relevance post-adoption or with organizational maturity.

Lifecycle Phases:
- **Discovery:** Gains are hypothesized or elicited from customer research.
- **Definition:** Gains are formalized, scored by impact and frequency.
- **Linking:** Mapped to related Jobs, Offerings, and Value Propositions.
- **Activation:** Used to shape messaging, prioritization, and delivery logic.
- **Validation:** Verified through customer feedback and outcome tracking.

Special Traits:
- **Satisfaction-Oriented:** Gains are experience and perception-driven rather than purely task-driven.
- **Cross-Solution Nature:** Gains often cut across multiple features or touchpoints.
- **Emotionally Weighted:** Gains frequently carry high emotional or social importance.
- **UX Signaling:** Presence or absence of gains shapes perceived product fit and customer happiness.

Fine-Tuning Parameters:
- **Persistence Score:** 6/10 – Gains evolve as customer context or expectations shift.
- **Influence Score:** 9/10 – Strongly affects customer satisfaction and loyalty.
- **Behavioral Sensitivity:** 7/10 – Realized gains alter usage patterns and feedback sentiment.
- **Cross-Domain Reusability:** 8/10 – Gains like “saving time” or “feeling confident” appear across industries.
