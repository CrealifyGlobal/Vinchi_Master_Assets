[[Class_Business_Structures]]

Class Name:
Business Structures

Behavioral Purpose:
Business Structures define how an organization is formally organized—establishing the authority, reporting lines, governance, and control mechanisms that shape how work, decisions, and responsibilities are distributed. These structures embody how the organization is *arranged* to operate—not just who does what, but *who is responsible for whom*, how oversight flows, and where decisions originate. Business Structures play a vital role in enabling or constraining agility, accountability, and performance.

They are distinct from Business Functions (what the organization does) and from Work Systems (how work is done). Business Structures set the *context* for both, determining who governs which function, how work systems are distributed, and how power and coordination scale.

Core Behaviors:
- **Organize Roles and Responsibilities:** Define clear lines of authority, control, and reporting for every individual and unit.
- **Enable or Constrain Decision-Making:** Structure determines whether decision rights are centralized, decentralized, or hybrid.
- **Support Strategic Alignment:** By mirroring product lines, geographies, or customer segments, structures help ensure organizational design matches business intent.
- **Drive Accountability:** Structure determines where performance responsibility and oversight live.

Interaction Targets:
Business Structures primarily interact with:
- **Business Units**, which are the operational or functional containers within a structure.
- **Roles**, which represent positions of authority, accountability, or contribution within that structure.
- **Business Functions**, to which structure allocates governance and control.
- **Decisions**, which follow pathways defined by structure (e.g., approval chains, escalation paths).
- **Work Systems**, influenced by structural constraints or enabled by structural freedoms.

Sensing Triggers:
- Changes in strategic focus (e.g., product vs. region, innovation vs. scale).
- Mergers, acquisitions, or major shifts in organizational size.
- Performance breakdowns due to unclear roles, duplication, or bottlenecks.
- Need for increased agility, cross-functional coordination, or compliance adherence.

Decision Rules:
- Every Business Structure must include at least one Business Unit and define decision flows across that structure.
- Structures with greater hierarchy depth (>5) typically indicate a centralized decision model.
- Structures must allocate governance for at least one Business Function.
- Decentralized structures must ensure dual-accountability or lateral coordination mechanisms are in place.

Adaptation Behavior:
- Move toward flatter, more cross-functional models when agility or innovation is a priority.
- Increase structural clarity when coordination failures, accountability gaps, or misalignment become apparent.
- Create hybrid or matrix structures to balance global consistency with local responsiveness.
- Modify structural boundaries during organizational redesign, digital transformation, or rapid growth.

Lifecycle Phases:
- **Defined:** Structural model (hierarchical, matrix, flat) is selected and mapped.
- **Instantiated:** Units, roles, and reporting lines are populated.
- **Operationalized:** Structure is used to make decisions, assign work, and manage performance.
- **Monitored:** Organizational performance and coordination are reviewed in light of structural design.
- **Reorganized:** Structure is redesigned to address inefficiencies, align with strategy, or scale growth.

Special Traits:
- **Enduring but Flexible:** Structures often persist through cycles, but adapt as context changes.
- **Power-Shaping:** Structure determines authority flow and decision rights—thus deeply influencing culture.
- **Integration-Oriented:** Structures define how different units, capabilities, or teams integrate to produce outcomes.
- **Context-Reflective:** Business Structures should reflect the firm’s operating model, growth stage, and strategic intent.

Fine-Tuning Parameters:
- **Persistence Score:** 9/10 — Structures typically endure unless intentionally redesigned.
- **Flexibility Score:** 6/10 — Structural redesign is significant and often complex, but necessary over time.
- **Influence Radius:** 9/10 — Affects nearly all operations, workflows, and decision environments.
- **Sensitivity to Change Triggers:** 7/10 — Structural redesign usually follows significant strategic or organizational events.
